The Handy-Dandy Scales Reference

Pentatonic Scales
Blues Scale (Pentatonic Minor) 1, -3, 4, 5, -7 This is a pentatonic scale over the Minor (Aeolian) scale. Note that you can add a cool-sounding flatted fifth (-5) to this scale.
Relative Blues Scale (Pentatonic Major) 1, 2, 3, 5, 6 This is a pentatonic scale over the Major (Ionian) scale.
Pentatonic Minor plus Pentatonic Major 1, -3, 4, 5, -7
plus 2, 3, 6		 This scale has an interesting relationship to the church modes. The Pentatonic Minor is a subset of the Aeolian (Minor) scale. The Pentatonic Major is a subset of the Ionian (Major) scale. The resulting scale has 9 notes, and in one place there are four half-steps in a row.

If you remove the 3rd (which just happens to be 3rd half-step of the 4 in a row), you get the Dorian scale. If you remove the -3rd (the 2nd half-step of the 4 in a row), you get the Mixolydian scale.

Whoa! That's quite a discovery!

So this scale is a convergence of Major and Minor Pentatonic (based on notes from Ionian and Aeolian modes), Dorian, and Mixolydian scales. Now that is a cool pattern! It is used (somewhat sparingly) in Blues and is a foundational scale for Rock.

HINT: You can use the BLACK/GRAY feature to distinguish the notes in the Pentatonic Minor scale from the added notes from the Pentatonic Major scale.

Church Mode Scales (Modern)
I. Ionian (a.k.a. - Major) 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 Played on the piano as the white keys, starting with C.
II. Dorian 1, 2, -3, 4, 5, 6, -7 Played on the piano as the white keys, starting with D.
III. Phrygian 1, -2, -3, 4, 5, -6, -7 Played on the piano as the white keys, starting with E.
IV. Lydian 1, 2, 3, -5 (#4), 5, 6, 7 Played on the piano as the white keys, starting with F.
V. Mixolydian 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, -7 Played on the piano as the white keys, starting with G.
VI. Aeolian (a.k.a. - Minor) 1, 2, -3, 4, 5, -6, -7 Played on the piano as the white keys, starting with A.
VII. Locrian 1, -2, -3, 4, -5, -6, -7 Played on the piano as the white keys, starting with B.
